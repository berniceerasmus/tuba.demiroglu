What are stative verbs?

Stative (non-action) verbs describe a state or condition — not an action. Because states have no real beginning or end, stative verbs are used only in simple tenses — never in continuous forms.

✗ I am knowing the answer.
✓ I know the answer.
✗ She is loving Istanbul.
✓ She loves Istanbul.
🇹🇷 Turkish uses seviyorum (present continuous) for love — but English uses simple: I love Istanbul. NOT I am loving Istanbul.

4 categories of stative verbs

These are the four main groups. Learn one group at a time.

💼 Possession & size
belong, own, have*, possess, weigh, measure
💭 Opinion & thought
know, believe, agree, think*, imagine, understand
❤️ Likes, dislikes & feelings
like, love, hate, want, need, wish, prefer, dislike
👁 Perception & senses
sound, hear, seem, see*, appear, look*, taste*, smell*, feel*

* These verbs can sometimes be dynamic — see the dual-use section below.

Dual-use verbs — stative AND dynamic

Some verbs change meaning depending on whether they describe a state or an action. When they describe an action, they CAN be used in continuous.

VerbStative meaning (simple)Dynamic meaning (continuous OK)
havepossess → I have two cats.experience → They are having a party.
beexist/describe → James is a doctor.behave → He is being very kind.
thinkbelieve → I think it's a good idea.reflect → She is thinking about her trip.
lookappear → Tom looks like his sister.focus eyes → Peter is looking out the window.
taste / smell / feelsense → This soup tastes great.test / action → She is tasting the soup.

Time expressions — your tense clues

These words tell you which tense to choose.

🟢 Present Simple

  • always / usually / often
  • sometimes / never
  • every day / week / morning
  • on Mondays / at weekends

🔵 Present Continuous

  • now / right now
  • at the moment / currently
  • today / this week / this year
  • these days
